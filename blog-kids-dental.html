Kids Dental Care

When Should Your Child
First Visit a Dentist?

Spoiler: much earlier than most Indian parents think. Early visits prevent pain, fear, and expensive treatments later.

Kids Dental 📅 20 May 2026 ⏱ 5 min read 👤 Dr. Ananya Kulkarni, MDS Pedodontics

In India, a common belief is that children don't need a dentist until they have a problem — a cavity, toothache, or visible damage. This wait-and-watch approach is one of the biggest reasons why dental decay is the most prevalent childhood disease in India, affecting over 60% of children aged 5–12 according to the Indian Dental Association.

The good news: early prevention is simple, affordable, and completely changes the dental trajectory of your child's life.

The Recommended Age for a Child's First Dental Visit

The Indian Dental Association (IDA), the American Academy of Pediatric Dentistry (AAPD), and virtually every major dental body worldwide agree: a child should see a dentist by their first birthday — or within 6 months of their first tooth appearing, whichever comes first.

💡 The "First Tooth, First Visit" Rule

The moment you see your baby's first tooth (usually around 6–10 months), schedule a dental visit. Baby teeth matter more than most parents realise.

Most Indian parents are surprised by this — they assume a child needs to have all their teeth before seeing a dentist. But waiting until age 3, 5, or "when there's a problem" almost always means the dentist is treating damage that could have been prevented entirely.

Why Baby Teeth Matter More Than You Think

A common misconception: "Baby teeth will fall out anyway, so cavities don't matter." This is dangerously wrong for several reasons:

  • Baby teeth hold space for permanent teeth. If a baby tooth is lost early due to decay, the surrounding teeth shift — causing crowding and misalignment that requires years of braces later.
  • Tooth decay is an infection. Bacteria from decayed baby teeth can spread to nearby permanent teeth that are still developing beneath the gum line.
  • Pain affects development. A child with a toothache struggles to eat, sleep, concentrate in school, and speak clearly.
  • Dental fear is learned young. Children who only visit a dentist in pain associate dentists with distress. Children who visit regularly from a young age see it as routine — just like a haircut.

What Happens at a Child's First Dental Visit?

At PureSmile Dental, a first visit for a toddler is gentle, playful, and completely non-threatening. Here is what happens:

  1. Parent Consultation: We talk to you about your child's diet, feeding habits (bottle, breastfeeding), oral hygiene routine, and any concerns you have.
  2. A Friendly Introduction: The dentist introduces themselves to the child gently, shows them the equipment, and lets them touch the mirror — making the chair a fun place, not a scary one.
  3. Gentle Examination: We check for early signs of decay, assess bite and jaw development, and look at gum health. For infants, this is often done with the child sitting on the parent's lap.
  4. Fluoride Application (if needed): A quick fluoride varnish application protects erupting teeth from decay.
  5. Parent Education: We walk you through brushing technique for your child's age, diet tips, and when to expect different teeth to emerge.

The entire first visit is typically 20–30 minutes and ends with the child leaving happy — often with a sticker or small prize.

Age-by-Age Guide to Children's Dental Care

0–12 Months (Before Teeth)

Even before teeth appear, clean your baby's gums with a damp, soft cloth after every feeding. This removes milk sugars that bacteria feed on and establishes the routine of oral cleaning.

6–24 Months (First Teeth)

When the first tooth erupts, schedule that first dental visit. Use a rice-grain-sized amount of fluoride toothpaste on a soft infant toothbrush. Never put your baby to sleep with a bottle of milk or juice — this causes "bottle decay," one of the most destructive forms of early childhood tooth damage.

⚠️ Never Share Spoons or Lick Dummies

Dental decay bacteria (Streptococcus mutans) is transmitted through saliva. When parents taste food before giving it to a baby, share spoons, or clean a dummy by putting it in their own mouth, they transfer decay-causing bacteria to their child's mouth. This is one of the leading causes of early childhood tooth decay in India.

2–5 Years (All Baby Teeth Present)

By age 3, most children have all 20 baby teeth. Dental visits every 6 months are essential. Use a pea-sized amount of fluoride toothpaste. Help your child brush — young children lack the motor skills to brush effectively on their own until about age 7.

6–12 Years (Mixed Dentition)

Permanent teeth start arriving from age 6. The first permanent molars (at the back) erupt around age 6 and are often mistaken for baby teeth by parents. These are permanent and need to be protected. Dental sealants applied at this stage can prevent up to 80% of cavities in these teeth.

12+ Years (Full Permanent Teeth)

By age 12–13, most permanent teeth are in. This is the ideal time to assess whether orthodontic treatment (braces) is needed. Early assessment avoids more complex treatment later.

Common Signs Your Child May Need Immediate Dental Attention

  • White, brown, or black spots on teeth (early or active decay)
  • Complaining of toothache or sensitivity to sweets
  • Swollen or bleeding gums
  • Difficulty chewing or avoiding food on one side
  • Visible holes or dark areas in teeth
  • A tooth that was knocked out or chipped in a fall
  • Thumb-sucking or tongue-thrusting habits beyond age 4

Tips to Make Dental Visits Easy for Your Child

  • Read books or watch videos about dentist visits together beforehand
  • Never use the dentist as a threat ("If you don't brush, the dentist will pull your teeth!")
  • Stay calm yourself — children pick up on parental anxiety
  • Schedule appointments in the morning when children are rested
  • Praise and reward positive dental behaviour
